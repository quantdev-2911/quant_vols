Numerical Test Configuration — Applied to All Scenarios

These are the numerical settings used in run() and test_off_grid(). They apply equally to Scenarios A and B. Scenario C uses its own alpha grid.

Parameter Value (exact from code) Reasoning
Log-moneyness grid k linspace(-5.0, 5.0, 10000) Range k ∈ [−5, +5] covers all economically meaningful strikes: at k = −5, the strike is approximately e⁻⁵ ≈ 0.7% of forward (deep OTM put); at k = +5 approximately e⁵ ≈ 148× forward. 10,000 points gives strike spacing of 0.001 in log-moneyness — sufficient to detect localised violations in the forward variance. Earlier testing with k ∈ [−3, +3] under-reported the wing deviation (gave 122 bps instead of 151 bps); widening to [−5, +5] captured the full wing behaviour.
Intermediate maturities per pair (n_inter) 100 100 uniformly-spaced intermediate τ values between each consecutive node pair, excluding the endpoints. This gives α ∈ {0.01, 0.02, …, 0.99} effectively. Chosen as a balance between computational cost and resolution — a violation that only exists for a narrow α range (e.g. α ∈ [0.44, 0.46] in Scenario C) would be detected with probability ~1 at this spacing.
Arbitrage tolerance (tol) −1e-8 Negative total variance differences below −10⁻⁸ are flagged as calendar arbitrage. This threshold accommodates floating-point rounding in the JWK→raw SVI conversion chain (which involves several sqrt and division operations) while being strict enough to catch economically meaningful violations. The counterexample has a violation of −0.0702, four orders of magnitude larger than this tolerance.
Alpha grid for Scenario C (n_alpha) 1000 1000 intermediate α points used specifically in test_scenario_C() to precisely locate the worst violation alpha. Finer than the 100 used for Scenarios A and B because Scenario C is the critical counterexample and needs accurate identification of the worst violation point for reporting.

Scenario A

Monotone Term Structure — All 11 Computed Node Parameters

Scenario A represents a typical single-name equity with monotonically rising ATM vol. It is anchored to representative single-name equity trade details from reference paper document the relevant sample-parameter discussion: ATM Vol 36.8%, Skew 7.2%, Kurt 9.4%, PutWing 76.8%, CallWing 66.8% — measured at the 3M expiry on date redacted. All 11 node pairs satisfy calendar no-arbitrage by design. The purpose is to demonstrate Finding 4: a 151 bps mispricing exists under implemented interpolation even without calendar arbitrage.

Generating Formulae (exact from code, lines 356–364)

sigma_atm  = 0.368 + 0.025 * (1 - exp(-t / 1.5))      # ATM vol, rising then flattening
nu         = sigma_atm²                                   # ATM variance
psi        = -0.072 / sqrt(t)                             # Skew: decays as 1/√t
kappa      = 0.094 / t                                    # Kurtosis: decays as 1/t
p          = max(0.768 - 0.060 * t,  0.20)               # Put wing: decreasing, floor 0.20
c          = max(0.668 - 0.055 * t,  0.18)               # Call wing: decreasing, floor 0.18
psi        = clamp(psi, -p/2 + ε,  c/2 - ε)             # Enforce JWK skew bounds
All parameters computed independently at each maturity t. No cross-maturity constraints enforced beyond psi bounds.

Reasoning for Each Formula Choice

Parameter Formula Reasoning
sigma_atm 0.368 + 0.025*(1-exp(-t/1.5)) Anchor: 0.368 = 36.8% matches the representative single-name equity 3M ATM vol from the reference paper document the relevant sample-parameter discussion. The exponential rise adds at most 0.025 (2.5%) as t→∞, giving a long-run ATM vol of ~39.3%. The time constant 1.5 years means the vol has risen ~1.6% by 1Y and ~2.2% by 2Y — consistent with typical equity vol term structure curvature. Upward slope ensures ATM total variance ν_t·t is strictly increasing, guaranteeing no ATM-level calendar arbitrage.
psi (skew) −0.072 / sqrt(t) Anchor: −0.072 = −7.2% matches the representative single-name equity 1Y skew from the reference paper document the relevant sample-parameter discussion. The 1/√t decay is the standard equity skew term structure documented in the literature (Gatheral 2004, SVI presentation) and consistent with the stochastic volatility prediction that skew scales as 1/√t under mean-reverting vol. This ensures the skew decreases to a realistic level at long maturities without going to zero.
kappa (kurtosis) 0.094 / t Anchor: 0.094 = 9.4% matches the representative single-name equity Kurt from the reference paper document the relevant sample-parameter discussion (at 3M, t=0.25: kappa = 0.094/0.25 = 0.376). The 1/t decay is faster than skew — kurtosis reverts to near-zero at long maturities. This is consistent with the central limit theorem intuition: at long horizons, the return distribution approaches Gaussian (zero excess kurtosis). It also ensures the denominator D = 2√(νt)·κ + ψ² stays positive, avoiding the gamma blow-up singularity.
p (put wing) max(0.768 − 0.060·t, 0.20) Anchor: 0.768 = 76.8% matches the representative single-name equity PutWing from the reference paper document the relevant sample-parameter discussion at 3M. Linear decrease of 0.06 per year reflects the empirical observation that wings flatten at longer maturities (smiles become more symmetric). Floor of 0.20 prevents unrealistically flat long-dated smiles. The linear form is a simplification — actual wings follow a more curved decay — but it is sufficient for demonstrating the interpolation finding.
c (call wing) max(0.668 − 0.055·t, 0.18) Anchor: 0.668 = 66.8% matches the representative single-name equity CallWing from the reference paper document the relevant sample-parameter discussion at 3M. The call wing is lower than the put wing at every maturity (negative skew: puts more expensive than calls), consistent with equity market. Decrease rate 0.055/year slightly less than put wing (0.060/year), meaning the skew asymmetry ρ = (c−p)/(c+p) becomes less negative at long maturities, consistent with empirical smile flattening.
psi clamp clamp(psi, −p/2+ε, c/2−ε) Hard constraint from the JWK parameterisation: β = (c−p−4ψ)/(c+p) must satisfy |β| ≤ 1, which requires −p/2 ≤ ψ ≤ c/2. The clamp enforces this at every node. At short maturities, the 1/√t skew formula produces large negative ψ that would violate the lower bound if left unclamped. ε = 10⁻⁶ keeps the parameter strictly interior to avoid the β = ±1 singularity.

Scenario B

Hump-Shaped Term Structure — All 11 Computed Node Parameters

Scenario B represents a stock with an upcoming earnings or dividend event creating a parameter hump peaking at the 6M (0.5Y) node. It demonstrates Finding 5: node-level calendar arbitrage arises from hump term structures, independently of the interpolation rule.

Generating Formulae (exact from code, lines 399–407)

sigma_atm  = 0.32 + 0.04 * (1 - exp(-t / 2.0))         # Gently rising ATM vol
nu         = sigma_atm²
psi_base   = -0.06 / sqrt(t)                              # Base skew decaying with maturity
hump       = 3.5 * exp(-8.0 * (t - 0.50)²)               # Gaussian hump centred at t=0.5Y
kappa      = 0.08 / t  +  hump * 0.20                    # Kurtosis = base + hump contribution
p          = max(0.45 - 0.04*t  +  hump * 0.50,  0.15)  # Put wing = base + hump, floor 0.15
c          = max(0.40 - 0.03*t  +  hump * 0.45,  0.12)  # Call wing = base + hump, floor 0.12
psi        = clamp(psi_base, -p/2 + ε,  c/2 - ε)
The hump function is a Gaussian kernel centred at t=0.5Y with width parameter 8.0. It adds to both kappa and the wing slopes, creating elevated parameters at 6M that fall off sharply on either side.

Reasoning for the Hump Design

Parameter Reasoning
Hump centre: t = 0.5Y (6M) Chosen to represent a predictable market event — an earnings announcement or fiscal year-end dividend — at the 6M tenor. For single-name equities, March fiscal year-end falls approximately 6 months from a September reference date. For single-name equities, the next quarterly earnings would fall in roughly this window. The hump must be at an interior node (not at the first or last) to create the ascending then descending pattern.
Hump function: Gaussian with width 8.0 The Gaussian kernel 3.5 · exp(−8·(t−0.5)²) gives a hump of maximum height 3.5 at t=0.5Y, decaying to 0.006 at t=1.0Y. Width parameter 8.0 was chosen to make the hump decay rapidly — it has essentially zero contribution by 15M (1.25Y). This is realistic: earnings-driven vol humps are concentrated around the specific event date and collapse quickly once the event has passed.
Hump amplitude on p: ×0.50 At the 6M peak, the put wing is raised by 3.5 × 0.50 = 1.75 above the base value. This large elevation (the 6M put wing reaches 2.18 vs the baseline ~0.43) is deliberately chosen to ensure the node condition fails after the hump descends — demonstrating that the production node-level monitoring would catch the arbitrage if it ran at enough strikes.
ATM vol base: 0.32 Slightly lower than Scenario A's 0.368 anchor — representative of a different stock, with gentler ATM vol growth. The hump is in the wing parameters and kurtosis, not in ATM vol itself — which is realistic: earnings events spike the smile curvature and wings more than the ATM level.

Scenario C

Explicit Counterexample — Raw SVI Parameters

Scenario C uses parameters in raw SVI form {a, b, ρ, m, σ} — not JWK form — because the counterexample was found by searching the raw SVI parameter space directly. Raw SVI is the underlying mathematical representation; JWK is a reparameterisation of it. Working at the raw SVI level avoids the nonlinear JWK→raw SVI conversion and allows more direct control of the m-shift mechanism.

How These Parameters Were Found

These were not chosen by hand. They were discovered through an automated exhaustive search of 200,000 random raw SVI parameter pairs. For each pair, the code checked: (1) both surfaces non-negative, (2) node condition w₂(k) ≥ w₁(k) for all k passes, (3) off-grid forward variance is negative for some α and k. This specific pair was the one that produced the largest violation (−0.0702) among all valid pairs tested.

Exact Input Parameters (code lines 452–453)

Surface 1:   a = 0.4153,  b = 0.1093,  rho = +0.3409,  m = +0.6907,  sigma = 0.0134
Surface 2:   a = 0.1404,  b = 0.7653,  rho = −0.0760,  m = −0.3828,  sigma = 0.4978
These are the exact hardcoded values from make_scenario_C_raw(). They were fixed after being found in the search and verified independently.

Parameter-by-Parameter Explanation

Param Surface 1 Surface 2 Meaning and Significance
a 0.4153 0.1404 The vertical offset of the total variance surface. Surface 1 has a much higher a (0.4153 vs 0.1404), which keeps w₁(k) elevated even far from ATM. This is what makes the node condition pass: even though Surface 1 has a much smaller b (narrower wings), its high a keeps the entire surface elevated. At k=0.125, w₁ = 0.456 — most of this comes from the a term since b is small (0.1093).
b 0.1093 0.7653 Wing amplitude. Surface 1 has b = 0.11 (small wings, tight smile), Surface 2 has b = 0.77 (large wings, wide smile). The large difference in b is central to the m-shift mechanism: Surface 1's narrow wings mean its total variance falls off quickly away from m₁, while Surface 2's wide wings keep it elevated far from m₂.
ρ (rho) +0.3409 −0.0760 Smile asymmetry. Surface 1 has positive ρ (call wing steeper than put wing — unusual for equity, but valid mathematically). Surface 2 has near-zero negative ρ (nearly symmetric smile). The rho values determine the asymptotic wing slopes: b₁(1+ρ₁) = 0.147, b₂(1+ρ₂) = 0.707 — so right wing slope more than quadruples from Surface 1 to Surface 2, ensuring the node condition passes at large k.
m +0.6907 −0.3828 The location parameter — where the smile is centred in log-moneyness space. This is the critical parameter. Surface 1 is centred at k = +0.691 (smile shifted right of ATM). Surface 2 is centred at k = −0.383 (smile shifted left of ATM). The total shift |Δm| = 1.074 is large. Under linear interpolation at α = 0.45, m_α = 0.206 — the interpolated smile is centred at +0.206, still to the right of k = 0.125. So k = 0.125 is in the left wing of the interpolated surface, which is much less elevated than Surface 1 at the same strike.
σ (sigma) 0.0134 0.4978 Smile curvature. Surface 1 has σ = 0.013 — an extremely narrow, nearly V-shaped smile centred at m₁ = 0.691. At k = 0.125 (which is 0.566 units left of m₁), Surface 1 is still in its sharp peak and has high total variance. Surface 2 has σ = 0.498 — a wide, rounded smile. The contrast between these two sigma values is what makes the m-shift create the calendar arbitrage: the sharp Surface 1 has elevated variance far from its centre, while the interpolated surface at α = 0.45 has a wider smile centred at a different location.
