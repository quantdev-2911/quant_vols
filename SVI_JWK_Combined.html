Understanding the model

The SVI-JWK model is a five-parameter algebraic description of the Black–Scholes implied-volatility surface. It was designed to express what the market actually shows — a skew at low strikes, gentler wings at high strikes, a smile around the money — in a form that traders can read directly off the numbers.

1.1 · Why we need a smile model

Black–Scholes assumes volatility is a single number. It isn't. If you back out implied volatility from market option prices, you get a different number for every strike and every maturity. Plotted together, these form a surface $\sigma_{BS}(K, T)$ that usually looks like a smile in the strike dimension and a term structure across maturity — hence "volatility surface". No-arbitrage requires total variance, not necessarily implied volatility itself, to be non-decreasing with maturity at fixed log-forward moneyness.

The prior production framework used the Iijima Smile model for this. It fits the market well near the money but flattens in the wings, and failed to calibrate in several shock records during the a stressed market episode COVID-19 sell-off. The SVI-JWK model was selected as the replacement because:

  • Its wings are linear in log-moneyness — a property consistent with Lee's moment formula for the asymptotic behaviour of implied variance.
  • Calibration is fast and robust — raw SVI is a five-parameter problem solvable by standard nonlinear least squares.
  • It is well-documented in the academic literature (Gatheral 2004; Gatheral & Jacquier 2014).
On a calm day, NK225 might trade at 16% ATM implied volatility at 1-year expiry. In a stressed market episode, this figure briefly exceeded 80%. At the same time, 1-year 70%-strike puts traded at over 40% implied volatility — a skew of roughly 60 vol points relative to a 130% call. The model has to fit this whole shape simultaneously.

1.2 · The Raw SVI parameterisation

The model does not work directly with volatility. It works with total variance, defined as:

$$ w(k, t) = \sigma_{BS}^{2}(k, t) \cdot t $$

where k is the log-forward moneyness:

$$ k = \ln\left(\frac{K}{F_t}\right) $$

so that $k = 0$ means at-the-forward, $k < 0$ is a put in the money (or a call out of the money), and $k > 0$ is a call in the money. Working in total variance rather than volatility has two benefits: calendar-spread no-arbitrage reduces to a monotonicity condition ($w$ increasing in $t$), and the shape fits Lee's asymptotic result cleanly.

Raw SVI expresses the total variance for a single maturity slice as:

$$ w(k;\chi_R) = a + b\left\{\rho(k - m) + \sqrt{(k - m)^2 + \sigma^2}\right\} $$

Five parameters $\chi_R = \{a, b, \rho, m, \sigma\}$ describe the whole slice. Each has a geometric role:

ParamRoleIntuition
$a$Vertical levelAdds a constant to the total variance — essentially where the smile sits vertically.
$b$Angle between wingsControls how open or closed the smile is. Larger $b$ means steeper wings on both sides.
$\rho$RotationBetween $-1$ and $+1$. Negative $\rho$ rotates the smile counter-clockwise so the put wing is steeper — the typical equity pattern.
$m$Horizontal shiftSlides the smile left or right. In equities this is usually small and negative.
$\sigma$Curvature / ATM smoothnessControls how sharp the minimum of the smile is. Small $\sigma$ means a sharp V; large $\sigma$ gives a rounded U.
For SP500 on a calm day, you might get $a \approx 0.015$, $b \approx 0.09$, $\rho \approx -0.75$, $m \approx 0.02$, $\sigma \approx 0.08$. The strongly negative $\rho$ is the equity crash premium: OTM puts cost more than equidistant OTM calls.

1.3 · Parameter constraints

Not every combination of $\chi_R$ makes sense. The raw SVI formula must produce a non-negative total variance for every $k$, otherwise implied volatility would be imaginary. This gives:

$$ a \in \mathbb{R}, \quad b \geq 0, \quad |\rho| \leq 1, \quad m \in \mathbb{R}, \quad \sigma > 0 $$
$$ \min_k w(k;\chi_R) = a + b\sigma\sqrt{1 - \rho^2} \geq 0 $$

The last inequality is the minimum-total-variance condition — the single most-often-violated constraint when the calibrator is pushed by stressed data.

1.4 · Why SVI-JWK: traders don't think in a, b, ρ, m, σ

Raw SVI is mathematically compact but opaque to trading intuition. When a trader sees SP500 has "got more kurtosis today", or they want to nudge the skew up by 2 points, they don't want to re-solve a nonlinear system in $(a, b, \rho, m, \sigma)$. The JWK reparameterisation replaces these with a set whose meaning is direct:

ParamMeaningConstraint
$\nu_t$ATM variance$\nu_t \geq 0$
$\psi_t$ATM skew$-p_t/2 \leq \psi_t \leq c_t/2$
$\kappa_t$ATM kurtosis$\kappa_t \geq 0$
$p_t$Slope of the put wing$p_t \geq 0$
$c_t$Slope of the call wing$c_t \geq 0$
A Japanese equity trader looks at NK225 and says: "Today the market is calm, so $\sqrt{\nu_t}$ is around 16%. The skew $\psi_t$ is about $-0.04$ — moderate crash premium. The kurtosis $\kappa_t$ is low because there's no event coming up. The put wing $p_t = 0.32$ and the call wing $c_t = 0.28$ — slightly asymmetric because the downside is priced higher." Every number is interpretable; no nonlinear inversion is needed in their head.

SVI-JWK parameters in differential form

For the differential interpretation below, write log-forward moneyness as $x$ instead of $k$:

$$ x = k = \ln\left(\frac{K}{F_t}\right), \qquad w(x,t)=\sigma_{BS}^{2}(x,t)\,t $$

The SVI-JWK parameters can then be read as ATM value, ATM slope, ATM curvature and far-wing slope quantities of the total-variance slice. This is an interpretation of the formulas already shown in this note; it should not be read as saying that every JWK parameter is literally a first derivative.

$$ \frac{\partial w}{\partial x} = b\rho + \frac{b(x-m)}{\sqrt{(x-m)^2+\sigma^2}}, \qquad \frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial x^2} = \frac{b\sigma^2}{\left((x-m)^2+\sigma^2\right)^{3/2}} $$
Param Differential / limit form Meaning
$\nu_t$ $$\nu_t = \frac{w(0,t)}{t}$$ ATM variance. This is a level quantity at $x=0$, not a derivative.
$\psi_t$ $$\psi_t = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{w(0,t)}}\left.\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right|_{x=0}$$ Normalised ATM skew. It is the first derivative of total variance at the ATM point, scaled by $2\sqrt{w(0,t)}$.
$\kappa_t$ $$\kappa_t = \frac{1}{2w(0,t)}\left(\left.\frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial x^2}\right|_{x=0}-\psi_t^2\right)$$ ATM curvature / kurtosis-type quantity. It is based on the second derivative of total variance at ATM, with the $\psi_t^2$ adjustment shown in the transformation formula above.
$p_t$ $$p_t = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{w(0,t)}}\lim_{x\to-\infty}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}$$ Normalised left-wing / put-wing slope. The minus sign is used because the left-wing derivative is negative when the put wing rises as strike falls.
$c_t$ $$c_t = \frac{1}{\sqrt{w(0,t)}}\lim_{x\to+\infty}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}$$ Normalised right-wing / call-wing slope.
The production rescaling discussed later in Section 1.7 changes the stored convention for the kurtosis parameter. Therefore, when reconciling these differential forms to production data, confirm whether the model-owner extract reports the original $\kappa_t$ convention or the post-rescaling rescaled convention.

1.5 · Forward and backward transformations

The model is implemented as a bijection between the raw-SVI parameter space and the JWK parameter space. Going from raw SVI to JWK:

$$ \nu_t = \frac{1}{t}\left(a + b\left\{-\rho m + \sqrt{m^2 + \sigma^2}\right\}\right) $$
$$ \psi_t = \frac{b}{2\sqrt{w_t}}\left(\frac{-m}{\sqrt{m^2 + \sigma^2}} + \rho\right) $$
$$ \kappa_t = -\frac{1}{2w_t}\left(\psi_t^2 - \frac{b\sigma^2}{(m^2 + \sigma^2)^{3/2}}\right) $$
$$ p_t = \frac{b}{\sqrt{w_t}}(1 - \rho), \qquad c_t = \frac{b}{\sqrt{w_t}}(1 + \rho) $$

where $w_t = \nu_t \cdot t$ is the ATM total variance. The inverse transformation (JWK → raw) uses auxiliary variables $\beta$ and $\gamma$:

$$ \beta = \frac{c_t - p_t - 4\psi_t}{c_t + p_t}, \qquad \gamma = \frac{\sqrt{\nu_t t}(c_t + p_t)}{4(2\nu_t t\kappa_t + \psi_t^2)} $$
$$ b = \frac{\sqrt{\nu_t t}}{2}(c_t + p_t), \qquad \rho = \frac{c_t - p_t}{c_t + p_t}, \qquad m = \beta(1 - \beta^2)\gamma $$

Two features matter. First, $\rho$ depends on wing asymmetry — if $c_t = p_t$, the smile is symmetric and $\rho = 0$. Second, $b$ scales with the sum of wing slopes — steep wings mean large $b$, which pushes against the Lee wing bound on total-variance wing slopes, $b(1 \pm \rho) \leq 2$, especially for long-dated slices where calibration can otherwise imply excessive asymptotic mass. This is the algebraic reason long-dated stocks are more prone to butterfly arbitrage.

1.6 · Arbitrage is not guaranteed

A volatility surface is arbitrage-free if and only if it admits a non-negative risk-neutral density (butterfly-free) and European call prices increase with maturity (calendar-spread-free). Neither raw SVI nor SVI-JWK guarantees this by construction. The parameters live in an unconstrained space but the no-arbitrage region within that space is proper and nontrivial.

Calendar-spread non-arbitrage reduces to monotonicity:

$$ \partial_t w(k, t) \geq 0 \quad \text{for all } k $$

Butterfly non-arbitrage is tested through the Gatheral–Jacquier $g$-function:

$$ g(k) = \left(1 - \frac{k\, w'(k)}{2w(k)}\right)^2 - \frac{w'(k)^2}{4}\left(\frac{1}{w(k)} + \frac{1}{4}\right) + \frac{w''(k)}{2} $$

The slice is butterfly-free if and only if $g(k) \geq 0$ for all $k$ and the wing bound $\lim_{k \to +\infty} d_1(k) = -\infty$ holds. This is necessary and sufficient.

The document_1 used Ferhati (2020) sufficient conditions on raw-SVI wing parameters. The document_2 switched to the Gatheral–Jacquier $g$-function. The documentation does not clearly reconcile the earlier sufficient-condition approach with the later $g$-function diagnostic. The third validation cycle should explain why $g(k)$ is preferred as an output-surface diagnostic, how it relates to the previous sufficient-condition tests, and re-run $g(k)$ on all available EOD grids since go-live.

1.7 · The production rescaling

Between document_1 and document_2, the production definition of the kurtosis parameter was changed from $\kappa_t$ to $\kappa_t / \sqrt{t}$. Consequently the auxiliary $\gamma$ becomes:

$$ \gamma = \frac{\sqrt{\nu_t t}(c_t + p_t)}{4(2\nu_t \kappa_t + \psi_t^2)} $$

(note $\nu_t \kappa_t$, not $\nu_t t \kappa_t$) and the forward transformation becomes:

$$ \kappa_t = -\frac{t}{2 w_t}\left(\psi_t^2 - \frac{b\sigma^2}{(m^2 + \sigma^2)^{3/2}}\right) $$
The rescaling is a material model change that occurred outside either validation cycle. document_2 checks that the independent validation team's independent implementation reproduces production at the 1e-12 level, but no portfolio-level PV-impact study was performed at the time of the release. This is a documentation gap for a regulatory submission and should be closed through an explicit PV, Greek and reserve-impact bridge.

Pricing-impact bridge and third-document_2 evidence

Arbitrage diagnostics and parameter-boundary tests are necessary but not sufficient. A third-cycle validation should show when a violation is merely cosmetic and when it becomes economically material for pricing, Greeks, reserves, and hedging.

Smile-regime diagrams and explanations

Skew-dominated vs Tail-dominated Markets

The smile can lean
or it can flare

Two different markets can show nearly the same ATM vol and yet price risk completely differently. The SVI-JWK parameterisation lets you tell them apart — raw SVI obscures the distinction. Both regimes are illustrated below with concrete parameters.

The key insight is that ATM tilt (ψt) and wing steepness (pt, ct) are independent shape dimensions. A market can be heavily skewed at ATM while showing almost flat wings — or heavily winged while showing almost no ATM tilt. Below are two worked examples at roughly the same ATM vol level (17.55% vs 18.91%), chosen to isolate each effect cleanly.

Why this matters for the validation

In document_2's §5.4.2 the front office overrides the US calibrator's wing outputs with the formula $p_t = \sqrt{\nu_t} + 40\%$ and $c_t = \sqrt{\nu_t} + 30\%$. Look at the numbers in Regime A above: the illustrative Regime-A call wing is $c_t = 0.040$. The hardcoded formula with $\sqrt{\nu_t} = 0.1755$ gives $c_t = 0.4755$ — about 12 times larger. This does not by itself imply that PV, vega or reserves are wrong by the same multiple, because the impact depends on strike, maturity, payoff, interpolation and the Greek being measured. It does mean the override can materially distort OTM call-side volatility levels and wing sensitivities. The third validation cycle should therefore quantify the finite-difference PV and Greek impact across representative OTM calls, barriers, autocallables / worst-of structures and any reserve methodology that consumes the wing outputs.

Takeaway on nomenclature

Skew lives at ATM. Wings live at infinity. They are orthogonal axes of the smile's shape. Neither of the two regimes above is "the right shape" — both occur in real markets, and a parameterisation that can represent both is a parameterisation that passes conceptual-soundness at a regulator. SVI-JWK decomposes a smile into these independent dimensions with five numbers; raw SVI can also represent both shapes but tangles the shape dimensions into non-orthogonal parameters that a trader cannot set by hand and a risk manager cannot decompose P&L against.
