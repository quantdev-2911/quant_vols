Model Validation Training Note · SVI-JWK Independent Benchmark

Why Andreasen–Huge is the preferred independent benchmark for SVI-JWK

A junior-validator guide for choosing, implementing, and interpreting a structurally independent benchmark for the third SVI-JWK revalidation.

1. Validator verdict

Conclusion

Yes — I would select Andreasen–Huge as the primary additional independent benchmark. The reason is not that it is perfect. The reason is that it challenges SVI-JWK along the right axis: it reconstructs an arbitrage-aware option-price / local-volatility surface from the market quotes, rather than fitting another SVI-family total-variance parameterisation.

For the third revalidation, the benchmark question is not merely “can another five-parameter or SVI-related surface fit the same smile?”. The better question is:

$$\text{Are the SVI-JWK weaknesses caused by the market data, or by the SVI-JWK parameterisation itself?}$$

Andreasen–Huge is useful because it helps separate those two explanations. If SVI-JWK shows wing instability or static-arbitrage issues but Andreasen–Huge does not, the evidence points toward SVI-JWK model-form or calibration constraints. If both fail, the evidence points toward quote quality, sparse data, stale marks, or inconsistent forwards/dividends.

2. SVI-JWK context: what we are benchmarking

SVI-JWK is a trader-friendly reparameterisation of raw SVI. It describes each maturity slice using five interpretable quantities: ATM variance, ATM skew, ATM kurtosis, put-wing slope, and call-wing slope. It is useful because it is compact, fast, and maps well to trading intuition.

But SVI-JWK remains a static parametric implied-volatility surface model. It does not guarantee absence of static arbitrage merely because the five parameters are inside their algebraic boundaries. The revalidation material already identifies issues requiring independent challenge: deviations of Japanese-stock wings from Totem consensus, worse long-expiry fitting for some U.S. stocks, and observed arbitrages especially above five-year expiry and at very high strikes.

Why SSVI / Deep-SSVI are not enough

SSVI and Deep-SSVI are informative, but they remain close to the SVI modelling family. They test alternative SVI-style functional forms; they do not fully challenge the more basic modelling choice: representing the surface through SVI-style total-variance geometry. A regulator-facing independent benchmark should include at least one methodology outside the SVI family.

3. What Andreasen–Huge does

Andreasen–Huge constructs an interpolation and extrapolation of a discrete set of European option prices into a continuum of option prices across strike and expiry. The method is based on an implicit finite-difference treatment related to the Dupire local-volatility framework. QuantLib describes AndreasenHugeVolatilityInterpl as calibration of a local-volatility surface to a sparse grid of options, and QuantLib-Python exposes both volatility and local-volatility adapters.

$$\text{Market option quotes} \;\rightarrow\; \text{arbitrage-aware call-price surface} \;\rightarrow\; \text{implied-vol and local-vol diagnostics}$$

This is materially different from SVI-JWK:

4. Why Andreasen–Huge is the right first choice

4.1 It directly tests whether SVI-JWK artefacts are model-form artefacts

The revalidation concern is not only calibration error. It is also whether the SVI-JWK form itself is causing wing shape, long-expiry, or arbitrage behaviour. Andreasen–Huge does not use SVI-style wing slopes or JWK parameters, so it gives a cleaner cross-family challenge.

4.2 It is aligned with the arbitrage question

The Andreasen–Huge paper presents the method as an interpolation and extrapolation of discrete European option prices into an arbitrage-consistent continuum. This is exactly the benchmark style needed when the model under review has observed butterfly or calendar-spread concerns.

Junior validator interpretation

If SVI-JWK produces a negative density in the high-strike long-expiry wing, do not immediately conclude “market data issue”. Reconstruct the same surface with Andreasen–Huge using the same quotes. If Andreasen–Huge remains stable and arbitrage-aware on the same quote set, the SVI-JWK parametrisation or calibration constraints should be challenged.

4.3 It works naturally with sparse / non-rectangular quote grids

Single-stock volatility data often do not arrive as a clean rectangular grid across every strike and maturity. QuantLib-Python explicitly notes that the Andreasen–Huge method can take a non-rectangular grid of option quotes. This matters for single stocks and long expiries where market data may be thin.

4.4 It is implementable without building an academic prototype from scratch

For a third revalidation under time pressure, implementation risk matters. Andreasen–Huge is available in QuantLib / QuantLib-Python, which makes it easier to reproduce, audit, and hand over than a custom constrained-spline or rough-volatility implementation.

4.5 It gives diagnostics that are relevant to downstream equity pricing

SVI-JWK feeds volatility-sensitive equity products. A local-volatility benchmark gives useful downstream checks: density positivity, local-volatility stability, sensitivity to sparse strikes, and whether the fitted surface can support exotic-option pricing without pathological local variance.

5. What Andreasen–Huge should not be used for

Do not overclaim

Andreasen–Huge should be used as an independent benchmark, not as proof that production SVI-JWK should be replaced. It is a diagnostic reconstruction method. It is still sensitive to input quality, forward construction, dividend/borrow assumptions, stale quotes, and interpolation choices.

  • It does not remove the need to clean quotes and identify stale or crossed markets.
  • It does not explain stochastic volatility dynamics the way Heston or rough volatility models try to do.
  • It should not be treated as “automatically correct”; validation should still run butterfly, calendar, density, and local-volatility diagnostics on its output.
  • Implementation settings must be documented. If different interpolation modes are available, the chosen mode must be justified and sensitivity-tested.

7. Real-world examples for interpretation

Example A — Long-dated high-strike U.S. stock wing

Suppose the production SVI-JWK surface for a U.S. stock shows a butterfly violation above five years and at high log-moneyness. The junior validator should reconstruct the same date and underlying using Andreasen–Huge.

Observed resultInterpretation
SVI-JWK fails; Andreasen–Huge passesEvidence points toward SVI-JWK parametrisation, wing constraints, or calibration procedure.
SVI-JWK fails; Andreasen–Huge is unstable or also failsInvestigate input data: sparse quotes, stale marks, forwards/dividends, inconsistent bid/ask, or outlier strikes.
Both pass but SVI-JWK fit is worseSVI-JWK may still be acceptable if within bid/ask and materiality, but document model-form limitation.

Example B — Japanese stock with sparse long expiry data

If Japanese stock wing parameters are manually marked or transformed from another smile convention, Andreasen–Huge can provide a market-quote-driven comparator. The validator should ask: “Does the market-derived arbitrage-aware reconstruction support the manually marked put and call wings?” If not, the issue is not merely calibration error; it is marking-framework and parameter-governance risk.

8. Test instructions for the junior model validator

  1. Use the same input quotes for SVI-JWK and Andreasen–Huge: same date, same underlying, same spot, same forwards, same discount curve, same dividend/borrow assumptions, and same quote filters.
  2. Build the Andreasen–Huge benchmark using option prices or implied vols converted consistently to prices.
  3. Evaluate both surfaces on the same grid: maturities, strikes, log-moneyness, and a dense wing grid.
  4. Compare fit quality: price RMSE, implied-vol RMSE, weighted error by vega or bid/ask, and number of quotes outside bid/ask.
  5. Run common arbitrage diagnostics: call monotonicity in strike, convexity / density positivity, calendar monotonicity, local-volatility positivity and stability.
  6. Investigate disagreements using the matrix in Section 7 rather than treating the benchmark as automatically correct.
  7. Document settings: interpolation mode, grid size, quote filters, day count, dividend assumptions, optimizer or calibration tolerances, and excluded quotes.

QuantLib-style pseudocode

import QuantLib as ql

calibration_set = ql.CalibrationSet()

for q in option_quotes:
    payoff = ql.PlainVanillaPayoff(ql.Option.Call if q.option_type == "C" else ql.Option.Put,
                                   q.strike)
    exercise = ql.EuropeanExercise(q.expiry_date)
    option = ql.VanillaOption(payoff, exercise)
    calibration_set.push_back((option, ql.SimpleQuote(q.implied_vol)))

ah = ql.AndreasenHugeVolatilityInterpl(
    calibration_set,
    spot_handle,
    discount_curve_handle,
    dividend_curve_handle
)

ah_vol_surface = ql.AndreasenHugeVolatilityAdapter(ah)
ah_local_vol_surface = ql.AndreasenHugeLocalVolAdapter(ah)

# Then evaluate ah_vol_surface.blackVol(T, K) and ah_local_vol_surface.localVol(T, K)
# on the same grid used for SVI-JWK diagnostics.

10. Recommended wording for the revalidation report

Draft wording

Validation selected Andreasen–Huge as the primary independent benchmark because it reconstructs an arbitrage-aware option-price / local-volatility surface from the same market quotes without using the SVI-JWK parameterisation. This makes it a stronger independent challenge than SSVI-family benchmarks for assessing whether observed wing or arbitrage behaviour arises from the SVI-JWK model form, calibration procedure, or market data quality. Heston and regularised Dupire/Breeden–Litzenberger diagnostics may be used as secondary checks, but Andreasen–Huge provides the best balance of structural independence, practical implementability, and direct relevance to the static-arbitrage concerns identified in prior validation work.
