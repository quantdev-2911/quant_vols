The trader's view — strikes, risk reversal, butterfly

The trader thinks in strikes as a percentage of the forward. The reference points that matter are the 25-delta put (OTM put), the at-the-money forward, and the 25-delta call (OTM call). The shape of the smile is then described by two numbers drawn across these strikes: the risk reversal (put vol minus call vol — the tilt) and the butterfly (average of wing vols minus ATM — the convexity).

Reading the trader diagram Wings sit at both extreme strikes — the left region (deep OTM puts, K/F below ~85%) is the put wing; the right region (deep OTM calls, K/F above ~115%) is the call wing. The "risk reversal" is a single number measuring the height difference between the 25-delta put and 25-delta call — it captures the smile's asymmetry. The "butterfly" measures how far above ATM the average of the two wing vols sits — the curvature. So "skew" to a trader is approximately the risk reversal; "kurtosis" is approximately the butterfly.

The SVI-JWK view — five parameters at ATM and in the wings

The model describes the same curve with five numbers. Each is a geometric property evaluated at a specific location: three of them live at the money (level, local tangent slope, local curvature), and two live in the wings as asymptotic slope measures of total implied variance, not literal slopes of the implied-volatility curve. The x-axis here is log-moneyness $k = \ln(K/F)$, which is the model's native coordinate.

Reading the SVI-JWK diagram $\sqrt{\nu_t}$ is where the smile sits at $k = 0$ — the vertical height of the ATM dot. $\psi_t$ is the slope of the solid tangent line drawn at ATM — it is small and negative, meaning the smile leans left (crash premium). $\kappa_t$ is the SVI-JWK ATM curvature / kurtosis parameter derived from the second-order behaviour of the total-variance smile around ATM — it is not statistical kurtosis of the underlying return distribution. $p_t$ and $c_t$ encode the normalised asymptotic slopes of total implied variance in the put and call wings. In this implied-volatility diagram, the dashed wing lines are visual steepness indicators, not literal volatility-curve slopes. Note that $p_t = 0.503$ is about 3× larger than $c_t = 0.168$: the put wing is steeper than the call wing in total-variance terms, which is why the left side of the curve rises faster than the right.

Trader speak ↔ SVI-JWK speak

Once the visuals are clear, the intuitive mapping between trader language and model parameters is straightforward, but it is not a literal one-to-one numerical identity. To answer your question directly

No — "skew" does not mean the low-strike region and "wings" does not mean the high-strike region.

Wings sit at both extremes — the put wing is at low strikes (k < 0), the call wing is at high strikes (k > 0). Skew is not a region at all; it is the tilt of the smile at the money, measuring how much the put side rises faster than the call side as you move away from ATM. In SVI-JWK these are three different parameters: $p_t$ controls the put-wing slope measure, $c_t$ controls the call-wing slope measure, and $\psi_t$ controls the local ATM tilt. A smile can have strong local skew $(\psi_t \ll 0)$ with relatively mild wings $(p_t, c_t$ both small) — a market where downside is expensive close to the money but very far-tail slopes are not especially steep. Or it can have mild ATM skew with steep wings — a market that prices tails on both sides more symmetrically. SVI-JWK makes this distinction more transparent to traders and validators; raw SVI also contains the information, but in a less directly interpretable parameter set.
