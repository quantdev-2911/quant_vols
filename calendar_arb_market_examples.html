Argument I

Absence of Observed Failure Is Not Evidence of Model Correctness

I
Model Risk Principle · Conceptual Soundness
The Structural Flaw Argument

The trader's argument is structurally equivalent to asserting that a bridge has no structural defect because it has never collapsed. In model risk, absence of observed failure has a specific name: tail risk. The model functions correctly until the conditions under which it fails are reached, at which point the failure is not gradual — it produces incorrect prices immediately.

Model risk guidance requires the following validation perspective:

"
Conceptual Soundness Principle

"The validation process should evaluate conceptual soundness... whether the model produces accurate estimates across the full range of conditions in which it might be used, not just those observed historically."

The test demonstrated that the interpolation rule produces calendar arbitrage under specific parameter combinations. The question is not whether those combinations have been observed — it is whether they can arise under plausible market conditions. The three examples below answer that question affirmatively.

Argument II · Example A

Single Stock Earnings Events — Single-Name Equity Book

The Mechanics of an Earnings Hump

Equity single-name options exhibit a well-documented structural feature around quarterly earnings announcements: the implied volatility surface develops a pronounced hump at the expiry nearest to the earnings date. This is not a stress event — it is a recurring, predictable feature of the single-stock options market for large-cap single-name equity names.

In the 2–3 weeks before an earnings announcement, the volatility surface takes the following shape across consecutive maturities:

Documented Market Structure Large-Cap Single-Name Equity — Pre-Earnings Term Structure

Near-Reference dated Expiry (Straddling the Earnings Reference date)

Implied volatility is elevated by 15–25 percentage points above the longer-dated level. The smile is sharp and narrow — the market is pricing a binary event, not a diffuse range of outcomes. Options buyers want the ATM straddle, not the wings. In raw SVI terms: high kurtosis (large κ_t), small σ (narrow curvature), smile centred near ATM (m ≈ 0).

Post-Earnings Expiry (One Maturity Further Out)

The earnings event has passed. Implied volatility reverts to structural levels, the smile is flatter and wider — lower kurtosis, larger σ. Critically, m may have shifted because the stock has moved by the earnings outcome.

Historical single-stock earnings moves for large-cap single-name equities average ±12–15% in a single trading session. A 15% earnings move shifts the forward by approximately 0.15 in log-moneyness terms. This directly affects the raw SVI location parameter β and therefore m in the calibrated surface.

The m-Shift Mechanism

Between the pre-earnings and post-earnings expiries, the combination of small σ₁ (narrow near-dated smile) and large |Δm| (location shift from the earnings move) creates the exact conditions identified in Finding 2 of the interpolation test as the failure mechanism for linear JWK parameter interpolation.

Near-dated: m₁ ≈ 0.00, σ₁ ≈ 0.05–0.10 (sharp, ATM-centred)
Post-earnings: m₂ ≈ ±0.15, σ₂ ≈ 0.25–0.40 (wide, shifted)

m-shift ratio: R = |Δm| / min(σ₁, σ₂) = 0.15 / 0.05 = 3.0

(Monitoring threshold: R > 10 triggers intensive off-grid check)
Earnings quarter with large move: R can reach 15–30
Illustrative parameter ranges — representative of large-cap single-name equity, quarterly earnings

Historical Context — Representative Earnings Cycle

A recent earnings cycle for large-cap single-name equities provides a concrete reference point. Several major technology companies reported results that substantially missed market consensus during that period, producing single-session moves of −15% to −35%. The implied volatility term structure entering these earnings dates showed exactly the pre-earnings hump structure described above.

In that environment, any desk pricing options at intermediate maturities between the pre-earnings and post-earnings expiries using linear JWK parameter interpolation would have generated a surface where the smile centre m was interpolated linearly between approximately 0 (pre-earnings) and a large negative value (post-crash). At the intermediate maturity, the interpolated surface would have its smile centred away from ATM — producing the calendar arbitrage mechanism at strikes near the original ATM of the near-dated surface.

★
Key Point

Earnings events are not unusual occurrences. For a book containing large-cap single-name equities, they occur four times per year per stock. For a book of, say, 50 single-name equities, approximately 200 earnings events occur annually. The question is not whether this scenario can arise — it is a structural feature of the market. The question is whether the interpolation rule handles it correctly.

Argument II · Example B

Fiscal Year-End Dividend Humps — Dividend-Season Equity Book

⚠
Important Disclosure

This finding is not discussed anywhere in the prior technical documentation. It is an independent independent observation applied to the market context of the single-name equity book. It constitutes an additional gap in the scope of the prior technical work, not a reference to content within it.

Background — Fiscal Year-End and Dividend Concentration

The corporate fiscal year ends on fiscal year-end for the majority of listed companies. This creates a highly seasonal pattern in dividend payments: cash dividends for a large proportion of the listed equity universe are paid within a narrow window around fiscal year-end. For stocks with material dividend yields — major trading companies, financial sector names, telecommunications groups — this creates a predictable, recurring structural feature in the implied volatility surface across maturities.

How the Dividend Affects the Volatility Surface

A dividend payment is a known, certain cash transfer from the company to shareholders on a specific future date. Its effect on option pricing operates through the forward price:

F(0, T) = S₀ · e^{∫(r-q)ds} − D_T

where D_T = present value of dividends paid before maturity T
Standard discrete dividend forward price formula

When T crosses an ex-dividend date, the forward price drops by the dividend amount. This creates a discontinuity in the forward price term structure precisely at the dividend date — and a corresponding structural difference in the implied volatility surfaces on either side of it.

The Moneyness Convention Interaction

This is materially compounded by the fact — established in the prior technical correspondence — that dividend-bearing single-name equities and single-name equities use different log-forward moneyness conventions:

Alternative convention: k = log[K / (S₀·e^{∫(r-q)ds} − D_T)]

Local convention: k = log[(K + D_T) / S₀·e^{∫(r-q)ds}]
The dividend D_T appears in the numerator for dividend-bearing single-name equities — shifting the entire moneyness axis at the dividend date. This interaction requires explicit assessment in any implementation review.

When the moneyness convention places the dividend in the numerator rather than the denominator, the entire log-moneyness axis shifts at the ex-dividend date. The same physical strike K has a different k-value for a February expiry (pre-dividend) versus an April expiry (post-dividend). In raw SVI terms, this is precisely the location parameter m-shift: the smile is centred at a different position in k-space across the two maturities, not because the market has moved, but because the convention has shifted.

Illustrative JP Financial Sector Stock — March Dividend Structure

Before the March Ex-Dividend Reference date (e.g. February Expiry)

Forward price has not yet dropped. The volatility smile in k-space is centred near ATM (m₁ ≈ 0). For thinly traded JP single-stock options, the smile has limited quoted strikes — perhaps 5 to 7 data points — producing a raw SVI fit with relatively small σ₁ (narrow curvature, constrained by sparse data).

After the March Ex-Dividend Reference date (e.g. April Expiry)

The forward price has dropped by the dividend amount. Under the local moneyness convention, this shifts the moneyness axis. The same physical strike maps to a different k-value. The smile is now centred at a shifted location m₂ ≠ m₁, not because the market has repriced, but because the axis has moved. The smile may also be wider (larger σ₂) because post-dividend, the event uncertainty has resolved and the market reverts to its structural vol level.

Consequence for Linear JWK Interpolation

An intermediate expiry — sitting between the February and April nodes — will be priced by linearly interpolating the JWK parameters between those two nodes. The interpolated m will be between m₁ and m₂, placing the smile centre at a location that does not correspond to the intermediate forward price under either convention. The interpolated σ will be between σ₁ and σ₂. This produces the m-shift mechanism: at strikes near m₁ (where the February surface is near its sharp ATM peak), the interpolated March surface may generate a total variance below the February surface — a calendar spread arbitrage.

Illustrative parameters for a JP dividend-bearing stock:

Annual dividend yield: ~2.0–3.0% (semi-annual payment)
Semi-annual dividend: ~1.0–1.5% of stock price
Log-moneyness axis shift: Δk ≈ 0.010–0.015 per payment

Typical σ for thinly-traded dividend-bearing single-name equity: 0.03–0.08

m-shift ratio: R = Δk / σ₁ = 0.013 / 0.05 ≈ 0.26

For higher dividend yields (e.g. major trading companies):
R = 0.025 / 0.03 ≈ 0.83

These are below the R > 10 trigger threshold — but the mechanism
is structurally present and compounds with the earnings effect for
stocks that have both quarterly reporting and concentrated dividends.
For stocks with large dividend yields and very sparse option data (small σ from limited strikes), the threshold can be approached

Why This Matters Beyond the Numbers

The dividend hump argument matters not because every dividend-bearing single-name equity immediately triggers the monitoring threshold, but for two structural reasons. First, it demonstrates that the m-shift mechanism arises from mechanical, contractual features of the market — not exotic stress scenarios. Dividends are known, certain, and recurring. Second, it identifies a gap in the prior technical work scope: the interaction between the moneyness convention difference for dividend-bearing single-name equities and the interpolation rule was not analysed in the technical documentation at all. This is an independent finding.

★

The interaction between local moneyness conventions, discrete dividend adjustments, and the linear parameter interpolation rule was not tested, not discussed, and not flagged. This constitutes a scope gap in the prior technical work, independent of the findings in the interpolation test.

Argument II · Example C

Pandemic Market Stress — Documented Stress Event

What Happened

On 9 the pandemic stress period, equity markets globally suffered one of the largest single-day falls since the 2008 financial crisis. The implied volatility index for US equity markets rose from approximately 25% shortly before the peak stress period to approximately 54% during the peak stress period, peaking near 85% in the peak stress period. This event is fully documented in public data sources including the BIS market-stress review (the relevant stress-period review) and equity volatility index term structure records.

The key point is what this does to the term structure of implied volatility — the relationship between near-dated and long-dated implied vol — and specifically what it does to the SVI-JWK parameters at consecutive maturities.

The Term Structure Inversion

In normal markets, the volatility term structure is upward-sloping: short-dated options have lower implied vol than long-dated options, because uncertainty accumulates over time. In the COVID crisis, this inverted dramatically into steep backwardation:

Maturity Approx. ATM IV (9 the pandemic stress period) ATM Total Variance w(0,t) Smile Shape
1 Month ~120–130% ~0.13–0.14 Sharp, high kurtosis, steep left skew, small σ
3 Months ~80–90% ~0.16–0.20 Flatter, lower kurtosis, σ increasing
12 Months ~60–70% ~0.36–0.49 Much flatter, wider, more symmetric
ℹ
Note on Total Variance Monotonicity

Observe that ATM implied vol is in backwardation (falling with maturity) but ATM total variance w(0,t) = σ²_ATM · t is NOT in backwardation — it rises with maturity because the maturity multiplier t dominates. This is why the node-level calendar condition at ATM passes even in a crisis. The problem arises at off-ATM strikes, where the wing structure is radically different between consecutive maturities.

The Wing Structure Divergence — Why ATM Passing Is Not Enough

The crisis creates a characteristic pattern in the smile shape across maturities that drives the calendar arbitrage risk:

Key Mechanism Off-ATM Calendar Arbitrage in Stress — The Wing Problem

The 1-month surface in the pandemic stress period had the following characteristics in SVI-JWK terms:

  • Very high κ_t (high kurtosis): the market was pricing a crash scenario specifically — a sharp, concentrated event rather than a diffuse range of outcomes. Everyone was buying OTM puts for immediate downside protection.
  • Large p_t (steep put wing): OTM put options were extremely expensive because every portfolio manager wanted crash protection for the next 30 days.
  • Small σ in raw SVI terms: the crisis buying was concentrated near ATM and near specific crash-level strikes, not uniformly across all strikes.
  • Negative ψ_t (strong negative skew): the put wing was far steeper than the call wing.

The 3-month surface had lower κ_t, lower p_t, larger σ, and less negative ψ_t — because the 3-month horizon priced in a range of recovery scenarios as well as continued decline.

The Specific Failure Point

While the ATM total variances satisfy the calendar condition (w(0,t₂) > w(0,t₁)), the wing total variances may not. The 1-month surface has very high total variance at k = −0.5, −1.0, −1.5 (OTM puts) due to the extreme put wing. The 3-month surface, despite being higher in ATM total variance, has a flatter smile — the wing values at k = −1.5 can be lower in total variance than the 1-month surface at those strikes.

Schematically (illustrative):

At k = 0 (ATM): w(0, 1M) = 0.13, w(0, 3M) = 0.18 ✓ 3M > 1M
At k = −1.5 (deep put):
 w(−1.5, 1M) ≈ 0.65 (extreme put wing from crisis buying)
 w(−1.5, 3M) ≈ 0.50 (flatter 3M smile)
 ✗ 3M < 1M

This is NODE-LEVEL calendar arbitrage at far OTM strikes —
consistent with the node-level stress scenario at 6M-9M.
The ATM condition passes; the off-ATM condition fails. Standard monitoring checks at ATM alone would miss this entirely.

The Implications for Linear Interpolation

Under linear JWK parameter interpolation, a 2-month option priced between the 1-month and 3-month nodes in the pandemic stress period would have its interpolated parameters (ψ_t, κ_t, p_t) somewhere between the two node values. The resulting smile would have intermediate kurtosis and wing steepness. But because the 3-month surface is already below the 1-month surface at deep OTM put strikes at the node level, the interpolated 2-month surface will also be below the 1-month surface at those strikes — confirming calendar spread arbitrage at the off-grid maturity.

⚠
Operational Consequence

In the pandemic stress period, bid-offer spreads in equity single-stock options markets widened dramatically (documented in BIS market-stress review). Dealers who could not confidently value positions at off-grid maturities contributed to this widening. A model that produces internally inconsistent prices between tenors makes this problem worse, not better — it generates incorrect delta and vega hedges precisely when hedging is most critical.

Why This Example Is the Strongest of the Three

The pandemic-stress example is the strongest because it is fully documented in public data, datable to a specific week, and the parameter movements are reconstructable from historical options data (historical options data, Bloomberg, CBOE). It demonstrates that the conditions for calendar spread arbitrage are not tail-of-tail events — they are present in the worst market stress of the past decade, which standard validation practice requires models to be tested against. Any model that fails under the pandemic stress period conditions fails a standard stress test.

Argument III

Internal Inconsistency Without External Arbitrage

III
Model Risk Principle · Not About Arbitrageability
The Model Arbitrages Itself

The trader may accept the market examples above but argue that the calendar arbitrage in off-grid options is not exploitable — single-name options at 14-month maturities are not quoted in the market, so no external party can trade against the model's prices. This argument has partial merit on the question of external arbitrageability. It does not address the question of internal consistency.

A Concrete Position Scenario

Consider a desk with the following two positions:

Position Maturity Grid Status Pricing Basis
Long call option 12 months On grid Calibrated to market quotes — accurate
Short call option 14 months Off grid Linear JWK interpolation — potentially mispriced by 151 bps

The desk reports this as delta-neutral. The hedge ratio was computed using the model's implied deltas. If the 14-month surface is mispriced by 151 basis points in implied volatility, the delta of the 14-month position is computed from an incorrect surface. The hedge ratio is wrong. The book is not delta-neutral — it only appears to be because the model says it is.

The London Whale Reference Point

The a well-known derivatives model-risk incident provides the clearest industry precedent for this mechanism. a trader was running a large credit derivatives book in CDS index tranches — instruments that were thinly traded or marked to model, with no liquid external market to arbitrage against the firm's marks.

The model used to value these positions was internally inconsistent — it assigned prices to different tranches that implied arbitrage relationships between them. Because the instruments were illiquid, no external market participant could exploit this. But the firm's own risk management systems could not see the true risk. The delta reported to risk management was wrong. The P&L was wrong. The hedges were sized based on incorrect sensitivities.

The eventual loss was a multi-billion loss — not because an external arbitrageur traded against the model, but because the model's internal inconsistency caused the firm to misprice its own risk, mis-hedge its own positions, and accumulate losses that were invisible until they were too large to contain.

Reference

Public supervisory case study on derivatives model-risk failures The mechanism — model-defined prices at illiquid maturities accumulating undetected risk — is directly analogous to the off-grid calendar arbitrage finding.

What This Means Day-to-Day

Every day, the desk sees reports computed from the model. For each of the following risk metrics, the presence of calendar arbitrage in the off-grid surface makes the report wrong:

  1. Daily P&L — The 14-month position is marked using a mispriced surface. The P&L attributed to it is incorrect. Gains and losses accumulate silently against the wrong baseline.
  2. Delta — Computed from ∂w/∂S on the interpolated surface. If the surface is mispriced by 151 bps, the delta is wrong. Hedges are mis-sized.
  3. Vega — Computed from ∂w/∂σ on the interpolated surface. The sensitivity to volatility is incorrect. The desk does not know its true vol exposure.
  4. Risk reports to senior management — Aggregated from the above. Senior management is making capital allocation decisions based on incorrect numbers.

None of these consequences require an external arbitrageur. The model is producing wrong numbers for all of them, silently and daily.

Argument IV

The Price-Setter Regime — A Higher Standard, Not a Lower One

IV
Model Risk Standard · Price-Setting vs Price-Taking
Absence of Market Price Requires Greater Accuracy

The trader's underlying argument is: "Because there is no market price to contradict my model at 14 months, the model does not need to be right." From a regulatory standpoint, this is precisely backwards.

There are two regimes of model use:

Regime Market Price Available? Model Role Standard Required
Price-taker Yes Fit to observed prices Standard — market corrects errors
Price-setter No Define the price Higher — no external discipline

At on-grid maturities (12M, 15M), the model is a price-taker. Calibration fits to market quotes. Errors are bounded by the calibration residual. At off-grid maturities (14M), the model is a pure price-setter. There is no market observation. The model's output is the only input to the valuation. No external force corrects errors.

"
Valuation Model Principle

"When market prices are not available, models may be used to estimate the value of financial instruments. In such cases, the model becomes the sole source of valuation and must meet a higher standard of accuracy and consistency."

The trader is arguing that the absence of market prices at 14 months means the model needs a lower standard of scrutiny. The regulatory framework says the opposite. The absence of market prices means the model needs to be held to a higher standard of internal consistency, precisely because there is nothing else to catch errors.

The Analogy

If you price a house at £1 million when the comparable market price is £900,000, the market will correct you — nobody will buy it at your price, and you will be forced to mark it down. That is the price-taker regime at work.

If you price a bespoke, illiquid structured product at £1 million when the correct price is £849,000, there may be no market to correct you. Does that mean your £1 million price is correct? No. It means the error is invisible until something forces it to surface — a trade, a counterparty dispute, an unwinding, or a stress event. Every risk decision you make based on the £1 million valuation is wrong. The absence of a market price to contradict you does not make you right. It makes you dangerous.

The Correct Response Under This Constraint

Because the system cannot currently switch to total variance interpolation (the theoretically correct rule), the following compensating controls are required and proposed:

  1. Daily empirical check: For each consecutive tenor pair, compute the forward variance Δw(k, α) at 100 intermediate maturities and 1,000 strikes. Flag any negative value as a calendar arbitrage violation. This is the only rigorous verification available under the current interpolation rule.
  2. m-shift risk indicator: Compute R = |Δm| / min(σᵢ, σᵢ₊₁) at each consecutive node pair from the raw SVI conversion. Flag pairs where R > 5 for enhanced monitoring. The counterexample that demonstrated calendar arbitrage had R ≈ 82.
  3. Wing divergence check: Verify that b₂(1+ρ₂) ≥ b₁(1+ρ₁) and b₂(1−ρ₂) ≥ b₁(1−ρ₁) at each consecutive node pair. These asymptotic slope conditions are necessary (though not sufficient) for calendar no-arbitrage. Failure here confirms node-level calendar arbitrage at extreme strikes.
  4. Heightened monitoring around events: During the 30 trading days surrounding earnings announcement dates (single-name equities) and the 45 trading days surrounding fiscal year-end (JP names), increase the off-grid check to 250 intermediate maturities and 2,000 strikes, and reduce the R threshold to 3.

Regulatory Position

The finding does not rest on the claim that calendar arbitrage has been observed in production. It rests on three independent grounds — theoretical (no guarantee, concrete counterexample), empirical (three documented market scenarios), and consequential (151 bps mispricing under normal conditions without outright arbitrage). The remediation (linear total variance interpolation) eliminates all three simultaneously and has zero additional computational cost. Pending system availability, the four compensating controls above are proposed as interim measures.
