Model validation note

SSVI Interpolation as an Arbitrage-Controlled Alternative to Linear SVI-JWK Parameter Interpolation

A step-by-step explanation of why SSVI is designed as a surface-level arbitrage-control framework, how it can be used between maturity grid points, and how to evidence the improvement with numerical checks. The note treats SSVI as an arbitrage-controlled alternative, not as an automatic guarantee without constraints and verification.

1. Problem statement

At the quoted maturity nodes, a desk may calibrate SVI-JWK parameters and demonstrate that each node is free of butterfly arbitrage and that adjacent quoted nodes satisfy calendar monotonicity. That does not automatically prove that linear interpolation of SVI-JWK parameters is safe between those nodes.

Validation issue: SVI-JWK is a slice-level reparameterisation. Calendar arbitrage is a surface-level condition. Therefore, the no-arbitrage test must be applied to the generated total variance or option price surface, not merely to the interpolated parameter vectors.

The alternative described here is to use SSVI, where the maturity dimension is controlled through the ATM total variance and a constrained shape function. SSVI does not remove the need for testing, but it gives a cleaner theoretical route because the surface form was designed with static-arbitrage restrictions in mind.

Important limitation: SSVI is arbitrage-controlled only when its sufficient conditions are satisfied and the generated surface is verified. It should not be described as automatically arbitrage-free merely because the SSVI formula is used.

2. SVI-JWK versus SSVI: what changes?

2.1 Raw SVI slice

$$ w(k) = a + b\left\lbrace\rho(k-m)+\sqrt{(k-m)^2+\sigma^2}\right\rbrace. $$

Here $w(k)$ is total implied variance and $k=\log(K/F_T)$ is log-moneyness. SVI-JWK rewrites the same slice using more intuitive quantities such as ATM variance, skew, put-wing slope, call-wing slope, and minimum variance. This helps calibration and trader communication, but by itself it does not impose maturity consistency.

2.2 SSVI surface

$$ w(k,\theta) = \frac{\theta}{2} \left[ 1 + \rho\,\varphi(\theta)k + \sqrt{(\varphi(\theta)k+\rho)^2 + 1-\rho^2} \right]. $$

The maturity driver is:

$$ \theta_T = w(0,T) = T\sigma_{\text{ATM}}^2(T). $$

The function $\varphi(\theta)$ controls the smile shape as the ATM total variance changes. This is the crucial design difference: SSVI links smile shape to the maturity direction through $\theta$ rather than interpolating five independent smile parameters.

Key idea: In SSVI, if $\theta_T$ is non-decreasing and $\varphi(\theta)$ satisfies suitable restrictions, the surface is designed to avoid static arbitrage. This is closer to a surface construction than a parameter interpolation shortcut. The practical conclusion is not "SSVI always passes"; it is "SSVI gives a literature-backed constrained construction that must be checked in implementation."

3. Arbitrage-control conditions used in SSVI

For a volatility surface, absence of static arbitrage requires both:

  • No calendar-spread arbitrage: total variance / option prices must be non-decreasing with maturity.
  • No butterfly arbitrage: each maturity slice must imply a non-negative risk-neutral density.

3.1 Calendar-spread control

For fixed $k$, require:

$$ \frac{\partial w(k,T)}{\partial T} \ge 0. $$

In SSVI this is controlled through a monotone ATM total variance curve $T\mapsto\theta_T$ and restrictions on the shape function $\varphi(\theta)$. A commonly used sufficient condition is:

$$ \frac{\partial\theta_T}{\partial T}\ge 0, \qquad 0 \le \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left[\theta\varphi(\theta)\right] \le \frac{1+\sqrt{1-\rho^2}}{\rho^2}\,\varphi(\theta). $$

With the usual limiting interpretation when $\rho$ is close to zero.

3.2 Butterfly and wing control

A practical sufficient set of SSVI butterfly controls is:

$$ \theta\varphi(\theta)(1+|\rho|) < 4, \qquad \theta\varphi^2(\theta)(1+|\rho|) \le 4. $$

These conditions are linked to admissible smile wings and non-negative density behaviour. They are not cosmetic parameter bounds; they are designed to keep the total variance slice within static-arbitrage limits.

4. How SSVI can replace SVI-JWK interpolation between grid points

  1. Step 1 — Calibrate or infer node-level ATM total variance.
    At each quoted maturity $T_i$, compute:
    $$\theta_i = T_i\sigma_{\text{ATM}}^2(T_i).$$
  2. Step 2 — Fit constrained SSVI parameters.
    Fit $\rho$ and $\varphi(\theta)$, or an extended SSVI version with maturity-dependent but constrained parameters. A common shape example is:
    $$\varphi(\theta)=\eta\theta^{-\gamma},\qquad \eta>0.$$

    For real production surfaces, basic SSVI may be too restrictive. Extended SSVI or constrained piecewise SSVI can be more practical, provided the cross-maturity no-arbitrage conditions are enforced and independently tested.

  3. Step 3 — Interpolate the maturity driver, not raw/JWK smile parameters independently.
    For $T_i < T < T_{i+1}$:
    $$ \theta(T) = (1-\lambda)\theta_i + \lambda\theta_{i+1}, \qquad \lambda = \frac{T-T_i}{T_{i+1}-T_i}. $$
  4. Step 4 — Generate the full smile from the SSVI formula.
    Evaluate:
    $$w(k,T)=w(k,\theta(T)).$$
  5. Step 5 — Verify the generated surface.
    Even under SSVI, validation should check:
    $$ w(k,T_{j+1})-w(k,T_j)\ge -\varepsilon, \qquad C(K,T_{j+1})-C(K,T_j)\ge -\varepsilon_C. $$

7. Why this is better than linear SVI-JWK interpolation

TopicLinear SVI-JWK interpolationSSVI interpolation
Object being interpolated Slice-level smile parameters such as ATM variance, skew, wings, minimum variance. Surface-level driver $\theta_T$ and constrained shape function $\varphi(\theta)$.
No-arbitrage logic Not arbitrage-free by construction; requires dense off-node testing. Designed with explicit static-arbitrage restrictions, though still requiring implementation checks.
Main risk Nonlinear mapping from JWK parameters to total variance can create off-node calendar arbitrage. Risk is reduced by tying smile shape to ATM total variance and parameter constraints.
Validation evidence Must demonstrate no crossing of generated slices across off-grid maturities. Can cite SSVI no-arbitrage theory and verify resulting surface numerically.
Best use Acceptable as an interim approach if dense controls are imposed. Cleaner remediation candidate where system design permits a surface-level construction.

8. Recommended validation test design

  1. Calibrate SSVI/eSSVI to quoted maturity nodes subject to no-arbitrage constraints.
  2. Interpolate $\theta_T$ monotonically between nodes.
  3. Generate off-grid smiles using the SSVI formula.
  4. Check total variance monotonicity on a dense grid:
    $$\min_{k,T_j}\left[w(k,T_{j+1})-w(k,T_j)\right]\ge -\varepsilon.$$
  5. Check fixed-strike call price monotonicity:
    $$\min_{K,T_j}\left[C(K,T_{j+1})-C(K,T_j)\right]\ge -\varepsilon_C.$$
  6. Check butterfly arbitrage for each generated slice using the density diagnostic.
  7. Report worst maturity pair, worst strike, breach size, and tolerance even when the result passes.

Validation conclusion: SSVI is not merely a different parameterisation. It is a surface construction designed around static-arbitrage controls. It is therefore a stronger literature-backed alternative to unconstrained linear SVI-JWK interpolation when the objective is to reduce calendar-arbitrage risk between maturity grid points. The conclusion remains conditional on satisfying the SSVI/eSSVI constraints and verifying the generated surface numerically.

9. Submission caveats and wording discipline

The note should be read as a validation explainer and remediation proposal. The claims should be kept at the following level of strength:

  • Defensible claim: SSVI/eSSVI provides a constrained, literature-backed route for arbitrage-controlled surface construction.
  • Defensible claim: Linear SVI-JWK parameter interpolation is not arbitrage-free by construction and requires off-grid testing.
  • Do not overclaim: Using SSVI automatically eliminates calendar arbitrage without satisfying its conditions.
  • Do not overclaim: The illustrative SPX-like example is empirical evidence from a specific market date.

For submission, the strongest governance position is that SSVI/eSSVI is a stronger design alternative because the maturity interpolation is tied to ATM total variance and constrained smile-shape functions. The final pass/fail control remains the numerical verification of total-variance monotonicity, option-price monotonicity, and butterfly-arbitrage absence across the generated grid.
